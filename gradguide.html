Before You Arrive

The Ph.D. program has two main tracks in the first year: Algebra and Applied and Computational Mathematics (ACM). You'll be asked to choose one based on your interests. For some people (discrete math) the choice may not be so clear. In this situation it is probably a good idea to contact a professor you may study with in the future and ask them which track they recommend.

Year One: First Fall Semester

Paying Tuition:

1. Don't pay if its not right.

2. If you have a stipend, normally you will only be paying for health insurance and technology fees ~$1000. Unfortunately, the exact amount changes every semester so its difficult to judge if its correct simply by the amount.

3. Even if you are dropped from all your classes for nonpayment, you can be added back into all your classes if you speak with the grad director or the graduate secretary.


TA Workload:

It's department policy that professors grade their exams not teaching assistants. However, if your professor chooses to ignore this rule, there is little you can do besides speaking with the graduate director.

Your TA duties should average out to around 20 hours per week. If you find you're spending more than this amount of time, discuss the issue with your professor or the graduate director.

Year One: Spring Semester

Getting Paid: Due to the complexity of the math department's graduate student payroll, its has happened that students were not paid on time at the beginning of the semester. Depending on how you feel about financial security, you may want to save enough at the end of the semester so that you can go without a pay check in the beginning of the semester.

Year One: Summer

Stipends are not guaranteed over the summer.

If you teach a summer course, you may not get paid until the end of summer.

Year Two: Second Fall Semester

Pick an adviser. It's ok to make an appointment and interview potential professors. The sooner you know who your adviser is, the better off you you will be.

Exams

Q: What are the qualification exams?

A: Two all-day exams: One on Analysis, One on Algebra or Computational Mathematics. They are usually given a week before school starts in the fall.

Usually the first year knocks out about half the class.

After you pass this exam you will receive a (very) small raise in your stipend.


You can find qualification exams from previous years on the math department website. Also many offices contain binders of old exams along with student solutions to the problems. Ask around, and look for dusty binders.


Q: What are the comprehensive exams?

A: Three all-day exams, plus a 1~3 hour oral exam.

While rare, people have failed these exams. It is better to approach them seriously, rather than as a formality.

After you pass this exam you will receive a (very) small raise in your stipend.

Teaching

Department Syllabi and textbooks are available from the undergraduate program administrator.


Supplies for classes, such as markers, chalk, and erasers are available in the math office.


When teaching Maple Lab, computers tend to boot up very slowly the first time you log into specific machine. This is also true for your students.


The graduate student copier breaks down or is missing paper often. Therefore the safest time for important copies is when the main office is open. After 5pm there will be no one to save you.


For large numbers of copies (tests, quizzes) its is preffered that you print a single page and use the copier.


Not all classrooms have chalkboards. Not all classrooms have white boards. It is suggested that you try to visit your classroom before you need to teach/lead recitation.

Miscellaneous

People are silently judging you for not responding to important email in a timely manner.


We are asked to close windows to the outside when we leave a room. Apparently open windows once led to a rat infestation for the stats department on the second floor.
